# Pedagogy — Dyslexic & Kinetic Learners

Apply these principles to every lesson built for a learner whose student file lists dyslexia and/or a kinetic learning style. Treat them as design constraints on the lesson, not optional extras.

## Core principles

- **Dyslexia is unrelated to intelligence.** Scaffold for patchy foundations, not low ability. Teenagers especially tend to have half-learned rules (punctuation, phonics patterns, fraction operations) that quietly drift. Build confident foundations early so momentum and self-belief build — self-belief is one of the strongest predictors of sustained engagement.
- **Multi-sensory, spiral repetition.** Revisit the same concept in new contexts across units rather than teaching it once and moving on. Do not assume mastery from a single pass.
- **Visual before verbal.** Open every lesson with a worked visual example before any block of reading. A short video before written work gives the "watch first" experience that real-teacher curricula provide.
- **Reduce text load.** Short lines, generous spacing, a large readable font (Lexend is the house body font), minimal words per page. Break instructions into single numbered steps. Never present a wall of text.
- **Vary the question format** within and across lessons — multiple choice, circle-the-answer, fill-a-blank, match, draw — so the page never feels monotonous and attention holds.
- **Write into structure, not into voids.** Note-taking templates use skeleton outlines, tables and charts rather than blank lined pages, giving a way to process concepts beyond pure writing.

## Kinetic / hands-on

- Build in something to make or do every lesson. LEGO is a genuine manipulative, not just play: one letter per stud, blending by pushing bricks together, building arrays for multiplication, modelling structures for STEM. Use it where the learner's file flags it.
- Tie learning to the lived environment. The Real-World Caravan Challenge block turns the day's concept into something done on the road (measuring, budgeting, observing, building).
- Prefer cheap, lightweight, multi-use supplies; one small supply run should cover a whole unit.

## Emotional scaffolding

- Acknowledge the learner's experience; for teens, build self-advocacy rather than just pushing harder.
- End each lesson with a low-stakes confidence rating and each unit with a reflection page ("one thing I understand now / still find tricky / a question I have / how I learn best"). These tell the parent where to slow down and give the learner ownership.

## Letter reversal & early literacy

For learners who reverse letters or struggle to retain the alphabet, use explicit multi-sensory letter formation, consistent directionality cues, and manipulative blending (LEGO / tiles). Spiral the same letters and high-frequency words through many contexts rather than expecting them to stick on first contact.
